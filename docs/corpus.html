Text Corpus Analysis

This page is meant to document my Text Comparison Corpus Analysis project, where I'll take a look at several different text files and compare and contrast their characteristics, like repeated phrases, common words, and how often they appear. The texts I'll be taking a look at are Bram Stoker's Dracula, Mary Shelly's Frankenstien, and Herman Melville's Moby Dick.The reason I wanted to look at these three books is because they all introduced literary characters who would become massive cultural icons, spawning countless references and adaptations. I thought it could be interesting to identify unique aspects of each work and see how they could have contributed to their modern relevance.
For this assignment, I mostly used the online program at Voyant and the AntConc software available for download online.

The first thing I wanted to do was check how often, and when in the text, the titular characters appeared. By using AntConc's Plot window and searching for specific terms, I can see the density of those terms' appearences in the text. First, I looked at Moby Dick, searching both for the terms 'Moby Dick' and 'white whale'.

As is visible in these graphs, Moby Dick is absent at the beginning of the story, until he's introduced by Ahab once the plot starts to kick up. He isn't very present for a large portion of the story, until the final battle when he attacks the Pequod for the final time.

In Dracula, the title character isn't mentioned by name that frequently, with only 34 mentions across the 250+ pages. This could be because he is referred to by his title, 'The Count', as seen with the plot frequency graph of 'count', though that could be a completely normal use of the word 'count' as well. Regardless, it's clear that Dracula himself doesn't appear too frequently in the novel. Instead, much of the book's gothic horror is derived from the nature of the world, a warped reality where the dead can rise from their graves and must be slain to protect the living. From a certain point of view, focusing too much on a single character as the 'main villain' could detract from that feeling of near-existential horror.

The word 'Frankenstein' appears surprisingly few times in the aptly named text, but this time there's a simple explanation. The creature called Frankenstein today wasn't given a name at all in their literary debut Instead, the title Frankenstein refers to the main character, Victor Frankenstein. As the book is mostly told from his first-person point of view, his last name isn't mentioned very much. Frankenstein's monster is called a series of derogatory names portrayed in the images above, like 'monster', 'creature', and 'fiend' (it's no wonder they cursed their creator).

In order to draw a conclusion from this comparison, take a look at all the plot frequency graphs together. The most noticable common thread between all of them is that in all instances, there's a large area of blank space where the character is unmentioned or absent, and this makes sense. I've always thought that a a great character is discernable when they can have an enormous impact without being explicitly mentioned or even present at all, and such is the case for these liteary giants.
Moby Dick doesn't physically appear until the book's climax, but he's present throughout the story as Ahab's main driving force. Moby Dick centers around how obsession can drive people to unreasonable and dangerous actions, and that theme is conveyed with how driven Ahab is about this whale which nobody else has even seen. In Dracula, the title character is but part of a larger issue -- the ungodly existence of vampirism itself. As Dracula dissapears from the plot for a large portion of the novel, the atmosphere of gothic horror is sustained by the world's uncanny features. In Frankenstein, it's essential that the monster be absent, at least for the very beginning of the story. Frankenstein is often lauded as the first true science fiction story, and in a similar vein to the sci-fi stories of today, it's important to establish the world the story takes place in, so that the implications of all the deviations from the norm can be fully appreciated.

To conclude this portion, I'd like to say I enjoyed how visiting these classic texts through this unique vise helped me appreciate each of their special aspects. In today's world we are experiencing a bit of an intellectual property draught, as sometimes it can feel like every company on the planet is looking for some near-forgotten character they can make a quick buck off of, but I feel as if that's a fundamental misunderstaning of what makes a character great. More than just recognizability, a character must be useful for the story their written with, ideally leaving a memorable impression while also making use of absence to make their presence felt even stronger.

Another method I wanted to emply was AntConc's N-Gram search system. By entering all three of my text files at the same time, I could search for repeating phrases and see instances from all files at the same time. With this method I should be able to see what groups of words all the texts use.

Some of the most common repeated phrases throuought the texts were common word-chains that you might not be surprised to see today. 'at the same time' pops up a good number of times in Dracula and Moby Dick, and slightly less in Frankenstein. This discrepancy could be explained by the difference in time periods the books were written in. Frankenstein was written in 1818, Moby Dick was completed in 1851, and Dracula was published in 1897. The gaps in years might seem arbitrary at first glance, after all, the gap in years from Frankenstein to Moby Dick is shorter than that between Moby Dick and Dracula, so what could be gleaned from similarites between the latter two texts? I believe that the discrepancy of the number of uses of certain phrases can be explained by the nature of literature as a medium. Much like other forms of media, books, poems, and the like are affected by the changing of the times, they're constantly shifting and evolving through different trends. It could be that between 1818 and 1850, when Melville began writing Moby Dick, that the medium of books changed enough that Melville's take on dramatic fiction would turn out far differently. If that's the case then, why are there many similar phrases in Moby Dick and Dracula? Was Stoker's novel just derivative? I don't think so. I'd bet that the shifting literatue culture plateaued between 1850 and 1900, much like most cultures do with time. It's what makes certain novels still accessible to today's reading audience, while other works aren't enjoyed by most. Surely we've all heard of No Fear Shakespeare, which rewrites classic Shakespeare stories for a modern, more casual audience. Would you be surprised to learn that a similar thing has been done with Frankenstein?

Meanwhile, Dracula has been retold again and again with virtually no changes to the original text. No, I mean, it's been literally virtually rereleased on tumblr.

This change in writing style, from what could be called 'outdated' to a modern writing style, could be what's being reflected in some of these repeated phrases we can see.
Of course, a much simpler answer to this little myster could be the books' lengths. Frankenstein has substantially fewer pages than our other texts, about 160-200 pages as compared to Dracula's 260 pages and Moby Dick's monstrous 350+ pages. I think that there are too many discrepencies in phrase repetition for a length-difference to factor in that much. Besides, it's much more interesting to discuss it from an alternate point of view, in my opinion at least.
